VP-Ellipsis/NCA Finder App for Romanian

The purpose of this app is the (ideally) automatic detection of verb-licensed ellipses in Romanian (the approximate equivalent of VP-ellipsis and Null Complement Anaphora in English). This isn’t realistically possible at this time, but it’s always worth taking note of what works and what doesn’t.

This app is also a PhD’s candidate tool for annotating elliptic sentences and trying out theories and explanations. As such, it is likely to remain a work in progress for some time.

1.     Parsing the sentence

The parse is carried out using NLP Cube, a statistical parser trained on the Universal Dependencies corpus that generates a dependency-grammar syntactic parse tree. Many linguists may not be familiar with dependency grammars – this choice of theoretical framework for such parsers was dictated, I believe, by practical computer-science-related considerations – so it’s worth highlighting a few notable characteristics:

  • First and foremost, there are no phrases – each word is a node in the syntactic tree, and each word (other than the root of the tree) is subordinated to another word.
  • A node/word may have any number of children/subordinates.
  • Function words are always subordinated to content words: the preposition is subordinated to the noun, the complementizer or relative pronoun to the main verb of the clause they introduce, the copula to the predicative nominal or adjective, etc. (This may be peculiar to the Universal Dependencies project, and may have to do with its multilingual character.)
  • For each subordinate word, the parser reports the dependency relation (abbreviated deprel) between it and its regent (e.g. nsubj for nominal subject, ccomp for clausal complement, etc.)

2.     Correcting the parse

This parser is a statistical parser, meaning it selects dependency relations based on probabilities deduced from the training corpus – it has no notion of grammaticality and ungrammaticality. It sometimes errs. For instance, it sometimes misidentifies an indirect object (the dative-case noun Mariei) as a nominal subject in the construction Mariei îi place (i.e. Maria likes). It also occasionally misidentifies words’ lemmas, which can lead to the app not recognizing an ellipsis, since the algorithm looks for specific lemmas.

The application provides the user two methods for changing the parse tree. The structure can be changed by dragging-and-dropping nodes. The details of each word (including the deprel) can be edited in the Conllu Item editor.

3.     Finding ellipses

The algorithm for finding ellipses works on the (partially valid) assumption that verbal licensers of ellipsis are verbs that take obligatory complements, and that the absence of this obligatory complement is what signals the ellipsis. Verbs like a putea (to be able to, semantically equivalent to can), a vrea (to want), a încerca (to try) all require a direct object. If the direct object is missing in the parse tree, the application will report a possible ellipsis. The lemmas the algorithm currently checks are vrea, putea, dori, începe, termina, continua, încerca, reuși, refuza, but the list can easily be expanded.

A missing direct object isn’t the only form verb-licensed ellipsis can take. There are verbs whose subject may be a VP in Romanian that license ellipsis, most notably a trebui (semantically equivalent to must). The application tries to identify one of the following patterns:

  • Intransitive verbs that license the ellipsis of the subject (lemmas: a trebui, a merita)
  • Passive-reflexive forms of verbs that normally license the ellipsis of the direct object (lemmas: a se putea, a se vrea, a se dori)
  • Impersonal verbs that express the attitude of an experiencer (specified by the indirect or direct object) vis-à-vis an action, e.g. singing pleases me (lemmas: a îi plăcea, dori, conveni, prii; a îl încânta, dezgusta, scârbi, bucura, uimi, privi)
  • Copulative constructions, e.g. it is possible/probable/difficult (a fi posibil, probabil, imposibil, improbabil, ușor, greu)
  • Copulative constructions with an experiencer indirect object, e.g. it is easy for me to do something (a îi fi posibil, imposibil, ușor, greu, scârbă, groază, indiferent).

The algorithm tries to identify these patterns in the syntactic tree and, if an instance is found and the subject is missing, proposes it as a possible ellipsis.

If the parser mislabels a complement, e.g. Mariei as a subject instead of an indirect object, the app will report no ellipsis, since it expected the subject of the verb a plăcea to be missing, and it isn’t. This is why it’s worth giving the parse tree a check if no ellipses are reported.

Ellipsis can be licensed by verbal constructions as well, such as avea chef (to feel like doing something, literally to have disposition), a da voie (to allow, literally to give will). Teaching the algorithm to recognize these is in the works.

4.     Suggesting an antecedent

Identifying the ellipsis’ antecedent is not straightforward. Nevertheless, the application gives it a try. The first step is to construct a “discourse tree” based on the syntactic tree. The app recognizes three types of relations, symmetrical (which is reflected in syntactic coordination between clauses), asymmetrical (which is reflected in syntactic subordination between clauses, introduced by conjunctions) and relative (relationship between a regent clause and a relative subordinate clause). In fact, determining if the relationship between two clauses is symmetric or asymmetric depends on the semantics of the sentence – it is possible to have an asymmetric (e.g. cause-effect) relationship between two coordinate clauses. The app works with what it has.

Typically, it has been observed, the antecedent can be found in the sister-node of the node containing the ellipsis, or at any rate in a node that c-commands the node containing the ellipsis.

5.     Feedback

If you try out an elliptic sentence and it doesn’t find the ellipsis, or incorrectly identifies the antecedent, please send the sentence to the author by clicking “Submit Sentence/Comment”. This will be much appreciated and will help the author.
